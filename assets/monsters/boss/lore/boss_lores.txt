Philippine Mythlogy:

Berberoka: A massive water creature from Philippine folklore known for draining rivers and lakes to trap unsuspecting fishermen. Once the water recedes and victims scramble for the stranded fish, Berberoka rises from the mud to devour them. It is one of the most feared creatures in ancient Filipino coastal and riverside communities.

Bungisngis: A one-eyed laughing giant from Philippine folklore whose name literally means one who is always smiling. Despite its perpetual grin it is a dangerous and unpredictable creature, so strong it can uproot trees with bare hands. Its upper lip is said to be so enormous it can be folded up over its entire head.

Anggitay: A female creature from Visayan mythology with the upper body of a beautiful woman and the lower body of a horse, similar to a centaur. Unlike malevolent creatures, the Anggitay is elusive and pure, said to be attracted to the scent of gold and precious gems. Encountering one is considered an extraordinary and dangerous event.

Dalaketnon: A powerful engkanto, or spirit being, from Philippine folklore who rules over lesser supernatural creatures. The Dalaketnon is described as supernaturally beautiful and lives in an invisible palace in the spirit realm. Despite its elegant appearance it is one of the most dangerous beings in Philippine mythology, capable of luring and trapping mortals in its world.

Norse Mythology:

Jotun: The Jotnar are the ancient giants of Norse mythology, primordial beings who existed before the gods themselves and inhabit the realm of Jotunheim. They are not simply large but represent the raw untamed forces of nature, chaos, and destruction that the gods of Asgard constantly struggle against. Many of the greatest conflicts in Norse mythology involve the gods battling or outwitting these ancient giants.

Fenrir: The monstrous wolf Fenrir is the son of Loki and the giantess Angrboda, and the most feared creature in Norse mythology. The gods bound him with the magical ribbon Gleipnir after he grew too powerful to contain by any other means, but Norse prophecy states he will break free at Ragnarok and swallow Odin himself. His jaws are said to stretch from the earth to the sky when fully opened.

Fafnir: Fafnir was once a dwarf who murdered his own father out of greed for a cursed treasure hoard, and his overwhelming avarice transformed him into a monstrous dragon. He was eventually slain by the hero Sigurd using the legendary sword Gram, whose blade was sharp enough to pierce Fafnir's seemingly impenetrable underbelly. His story is a warning about the corrupting and transforming power of unchecked greed.

Sleipnir: Sleipnir is the eight-legged divine horse of Odin, the fastest being in all nine realms capable of traveling across land, sea, and through the air. He was born of Loki who had transformed into a mare to distract the giant horse Svadilfari, and was given to Odin as the greatest of all horses. No living creature can outrun Sleipnir, and he carried Odin across the cosmos and even into the realm of the dead.

Huginn and Muninn: Huginn and Muninn, meaning Thought and Memory, are the two divine ravens of Odin who fly across all nine realms every day and return to whisper everything they have seen into his ears. They are the eyes and ears of the Allfather, and Odin has confessed that he fears losing Muninn, Memory, above all else. To fight them is to fight the very knowledge and consciousness of the god who sees all things.

Greek Mythology:

Cerberus: Cerberus is the three-headed hound who guards the entrance to the Greek underworld, preventing the dead from leaving and the living from entering. He is the offspring of the monsters Typhon and Echidna and serves Hades faithfully. In the rare myths where heroes bypass him, they do so through music, sleep-inducing honey cakes, or sheer strength as Heracles demonstrated when he dragged Cerberus out of the underworld with his bare hands.

Hydra: The Lernaean Hydra is a serpentine water monster that lived in the swamps of Lerna in ancient Greece, with multiple heads that would regrow as two whenever one was severed. Slaying it was the second of Heracles' twelve labors, and he could only succeed by having his companion Iolaus cauterize each stump before new heads could grow. The Hydra's blood was so venomous that even its footprints were said to be poisonous.

Medusa: Medusa was once a beautiful mortal woman transformed into a terrifying Gorgon by Athena as punishment. Her gaze could turn any living creature who met her eyes directly into stone, making her nearly impossible to confront. She was eventually slain by the hero Perseus who used a mirrored shield to look at her reflection rather than directly at her, then severed her head with the divine blade Harpe given to him by Hermes.
